Bike components

New to bicycles? Don't worry! This article will serve as a guide to introduce you to the basic parts of a bike. Without further ado, let's get started.

Overview of a bike anatomy

Make reference to the diagram below for the different parts of the bicycle:

Detailed bike anatomy
Fork Attached to the front wheel, the fork can include suspension or be rigid according to the bike classification.
Head tube Connects the fork and the rest of the frame, and is also the connecting point for the headset.
Top tube Running along the top of the frame, it usually consists of rear brake cables that are integrated within it.
Down tube Bottom of the frame which connects to the head tube.
Bottom bracket Refers to the bottom of the frame, where the parts of the drivetrain are connected.
Seat tube Vertical tube of the frame that connects the top/down tube, and the bottom bracket.

Seat stay Extends from the seat tube to meet the chain stay at the point where the rear wheel connects to the frame.
Handlebars Bars that are used for steering the bike via turning the fork, and could either have drops or be completely flat, depending on the bike type.
Seatpost This slots into the seat tube of the frame, and is where you attach the saddle (usually fastened by a seat release/bolt).
Saddle The seat of the bike.
Brake/gear levers Attached to the handlebars, and gear levers are sometimes built into the brake levers as well. Pulling the lever will trigger braking, whilst moving it side to side will cause a gear shift.
Chainset Attached to the bottom bracket, and usually consists of gears at front of bike.
Cranks Attached to the chainset, and are the arms to which the pedals are attached.
Pedals Footrest to enable the user to pedal the bike, and can either be flat or clip-in.
Chain Wraps around the chainsets and gears at the rear, and is turned by pedalling motion.
Front derailleur Only present in bikes with double or triple chainset, since it moves the chain up and down the rings using the cables attached to the gear levers.
Cassette Gear cogs at the back of the bike which the chain wraps around.
Rear derailleur Similar function to the front derailleur. Screws are present in derailleurs that can be tightened or loosened to make gear changes more efficient.
Jockey wheels Minor cogs that the chain runs around underneath the cassette/derailleur, which allows it to keep moving smoothly on its return to the chainset.
Spokes Thin metal rods that make up the centre of the wheel, providing strength and structure to it.
Quick release skewer A lever present in most modern bikes that allows for the removal of wheels without the use of tools.
Wheel hub Centre of the wheel, which contains bearings that enable smooth turning of the wheel.
Stem A feature on road bikes that enable the handlebars to be situated further forwards than the top of the head tube.
Headset Provides the smooth connecting interface between the head tube and the front fork that enables the fork to rotate, and you to steer the bike. Spacers (metal rings) can be added to raise the bars for a more comfortable riding position.
Brakes Actual braking mechanisms that are triggered by pulling the brake levers. Consists of disc, rim or drum brakes.
Brake pads Attached to the brakes, they push against the surface of the wheel when the levers are pulled, causing the bike to stop.
Disc rotor The actual disc that is being stopped when disc brakes are triggered.
Rear shock Present in some mountain bikes, this adds comfort and control when going downhill, and over obstacles or jumps.
