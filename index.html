Basic bicycle maintenance tips for beginners

Bicycles, like many of the tools we use in our day-to-day lives, require some care to maintain. If you're looking to buy an expensive bike, maintenance then becomes of significant importance. Here are some tips to maximise your bike's shelf, life, and ensure that it does not rust or break down prematurely.

1. Consistently clean and lubricate the drivetrain.

Does your bike make squeaky sounds when you are riding it? That's probably a sign that the lubrication has worn off from the bike. It causes the metallic chains to rub against dirt, along with the other metal components of the drivetrain. Such chains are rather expensive, so you would be saving yourself a lot of trouble by regularly cleaning and lubricating the drivetrain. Here are some tools that you will need for this activity:

  • Disposable rags
  • Bike oil
  • Degreaser product
  • A toothbrush or something else to pry off residue

After cleaning the drivetrain with the rags and toothbrush, turn the crank slowly backwards and simultaneously apply a drop of bike oil on the inside of every link on your chain. You could also clean the frame and wheels while you're at it.

2. Keep your tires inflated.

The tire pressure plays a tremendous role in ensuring the quality of your bike ride. If the pressure is too low, it would take a lot more effort to keep the same speed, as compared to with a proper high pressure. You will also be more likely to hit flats, especially when hitting a curb hard. An extremely important tool you should have is a quality floor pump with a pressure gauge. You could check the suggested pressure on the side of your tires. The rear tire should have more pressure than the front, since it is taking more of the rider's weight. Ensure than you do check your tire pressure once a fortnight.

If you are not going to ride your bicycle for a long time, such as a couple of months, try to remember to keep the tires inflated even during the pause. If this is not possible, you should take the tires off completely. This ensures that cracks on the sides of the tires are prevented.

3. Keep the nuts and bolts tight, but do not overtighten them.

Keep the screws, bolts and nuts in your bike where they belong by regularly checking if they are loose. Note that some newer bike models have a torque limit written on them, and that you can buy tools that only apply a specific amount of torque.

4. Ensure that the brakes are adjusted correctly, and change the brake pads if necessary.

For some of you fast cyclists out there, it is pivotal to ensure that you stop effectively when needed to avoid nasty accidents from happening. If your bike fails to slow down quickly even after you have pulled the brake handle to touch the handlebar, you may have to adjust the brakes closer to the rim or the disc.

You could tighten the adjusting barrel, if your brakes have one, in the brake lever or brake arm end. You may also need to unscrew the bolt holding the wire, tighten the wire, before screwing the bolt back on. Before tightening the bolt again, twist the adjuster holding the wire and the wire housing to the loosest setting. Also note that you should keep both the pads and the braking surface clean from dirt and oil, to reducing wear and tear of the brake components.
