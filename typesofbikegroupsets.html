Groupsets at a glance

A bike's groupset consists of shifters, ca chainset, a cassette, deraileurs, brakes, a bottom bracket, a chain, and cables. There are 3 leading groupset manufacturers, namely Shimano, SRAM and Campagnolo. Groupsets are often arranged into heirarchies, with compatible parts grouped in order of quality and price.

Groupset heirarchies explained

The level of constant research and devlopment of groupsets often come at a cost, and elite components consisting of superior quality can often be expensive. However, lowe groupsets also continue to be updated with many breakthroughs of the higher tier products.

Differentiating between different brands

Campagnolo creates components that are knwon for their satisfying mechanical "clunks", sensational precision, and artistic apporoach to their product finish, design and activation. It also solely focuses on road bike components, which some feel adds to the quality of their mechanisms.

Shimano, on the other hand, is one of the industry's biggest players. This means that it is almost standard issue in some quarters, and that its components tend to be compatible with a wide range of systems and bikes. Its shifting is slicker than its 2 rivals, while the brand is typified by quality and performance across its different heirarchies.

Finally, for SRAM, its selling point is its dedication to constant innovation. Their groupsets are also highly sought after for their industry-leading weight-saving properties for both road and MTBs.

Types of groupsets

MTB groupsets

The MTB bike groupset market is dominated primarily by Shimano and SRAM, with Shimano boasting about 8 complete sets, and SRAM 9. MTB groupsets are generally designed to be tougher and more rugged to handle the rigours of the trail, which is a trade-off from the finesse that road components offer. 1X systems have gathered huge popularity over the years, while triple chainsets have instead experienced a plunge in usage.

Road groupsets

Road groupsets are designed for one purpose in mind - speed. Such groupsets are built upon lean, clean and obscenely fast shifting machines. Gear ratios are also tighter, with speed and graduated climbing being the focus. 2X chainsets dominate the market, although 1X chainsets are embraced by a few who appreciate their weight-saving properties. All 3 manufacturers have also successfully introduced electronic shifting for their higher-end groupsets.

Conclusion

Upgrading components one at a time may require specialist knowledge, since most components are designed to work best within their brands and heirarchies. As such, it is best to stay in the brand. At the same time, ensure that the parts are of the same gear configuration. To decide on a groupset, establish a budget, and also ensure that the bike groupset fits your bike type.
